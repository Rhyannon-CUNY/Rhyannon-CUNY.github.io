Red states would lose the most if Trump cuts federal disaster support

The most disaster-hit communities continue to vote for Trump, but he could cut federal disaster funds

By Rhyannon Bartlett-Imadegawa

December 13, 2024


Republican states in the south receive the most federal disaster funds and would suffer the most if president-elect Donald Trump cut federal disaster relief, according to an analysis of data. Some of the most climate vulnerable communities who are least resilient are located there, but continue to vote for Trump.

Texas, Florida, and Louisiana suffered the highest costs from weather and climate disasters between 1980 and 2024, according to data from the NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI). The dataset included climate or weather disasters where overall damages and costs were estimated to reach or exceed $1 billion.

“Did you ever notice the red states get the most money or have the most disasters?” points out Craig Fugate, former FEMA administrator under the Obama Presidency. “If you look at states based upon just who gets the most disasters, and you go how much of that is in a Republican-led state where they control the legislature, Governor, you find it's interesting.”

Trump has regularly questioned the impact of climate change. Project 2025, a blueprint for his second administration offered by the Heritage Foundation, recommends that the Federal Emergency Management Agency(FEMA), be reformed with a “greater emphasis on federalism." Namely shifting the "majority of preparedness and response costs" to states and local authorities.

It calls for the federal government only covering 25% in the case of smaller disasters. The proportion would then rise up to 75% for catastrophic cases, but that would be at the discretion of the executive: Trump.

Although Trump distanced himself from the policy paper during the election campaign, in recent days he has called parts of it "very conservative and very good." He has also nominated several people with ties to Project 2025 to cabinet roles.

“I would be fascinated to know what Texas would think, what Oklahoma would think, what Louisiana would think, what Florida would think, if they suddenly had the majority of their disaster [support] slashed to 25%,” says Fugate.

The increasing frequency of catastrophic weather events is also a concern, draining resources and the ability to recover or plan between disasters.

Federal funding is only unlocked once local and state resources are overwhelmed. Samantha Penta, Associate Professor at the Department of Emergency Management and Homeland Security is concerned that removing federal support could “leave communities and states hanging when they need help the most.”

In a measure of climate vulnerability that takes into account risk and resilience, the nonprofit Environmental Defence Fund found seven of the top 20 most vulnerable counties in the country are in Louisiana. All but one voted for Trump in 2024.

Calcasieu Parish in Louisiana is one of them. When it was hit by devastating Hurricanes Laura and Delta in 2020, it was estimated 50% of residences, or 44,000 housing units, were damaged, a report by McKinsey & Company found. That is not to mention the impact on people's lives.

FEMA provided $417 million in public assistance obligations relating to Hurricane Laura, and an additional $1.7 million for Hurricane Delta, according to analysis by Rebuild by Design, part of the Institute for Public Knowledge at New York University. They also found Louisiana had the highest per capita disaster assistance in the U.S., at $2,681.

If they slash federal funding but do not mitigate by local governments insuring more of their buildings, for example, “you're going to leave a lot of very poor communities extremely vulnerable,” warns Fugate.
